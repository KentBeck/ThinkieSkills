I once had a coaching student, one of the brightest I ever taught, who simply couldn’t switch between scales. Put them in a situation with a task & they could blaze through it, accumulating tremendous context in their head & manipulating it. But jumping from that scale to the next scale up or down? Instant confusion.

Staying stuck at one scale was becoming a bottleneck. They had gotten so good at completing tasks that they could finish one essentially instantly. Picking the next task? Took (in comparison) forever. I tried every explanation I could think of.

We were both getting frustrated. I was at a loss for how to help. I’d wake up nights trying to find an exercise that might help (I take my coaching seriously). Finally, just before the engagement ended, I had an idea.

The Idea

We had discussed the idea of switching scales in the abstract. It felt like describing color to someone color-blind (like me). I acknowledge that what you are describing is real for you, just not for me.

We had spent much of our time in coaching sessions refactoring. Better design skills would give even more leverage to their considerable coding skills. One way their scale-blindness manifested was they couldn’t switch quickly from the inside of a function to the callers of the function.

We were refactoring a function to add 3 parameters. I saw my chance. Their impulse was to add all 3 parameters to the function, then go to the callers & add all 3 parameters to each. After the first parameter, though, I put my hand over the keyboard. “Go add this one to all the callers.” “But wouldn’t it be more efficient…” “Do it.”

Reluctantly they added the parameter to the callers. It went quickly & confidently since there was only one change. Then they added the next parameter to the function. It went fast. Then added it to the callers. Then the third parameter…

“…Oh.” At that, as they say in Zen, the student was enlightened.

Switching Scope With Genies

The ability to hop between scopes has always been valuable in software development. Problems unsolvable at one level are often trivial at another—

  • Can’t get this test to pass? Maybe we have the wrong test.

  • Feature proving too expensive? Maybe we can tweak the feature to catch most of the value for a fraction of the cost.

  • Feature proving too expensive? Maybe there’s a different structure that would make it easy & we should tidy first.

There’s More

The argument for smooth scope switching isn’t primarily “costs less” efficiency. Each scope switch potentially creates value. Switching to a smaller scope:

  • Generates feedback about decisions made at larger scopes.

  • Realizes the value of those decisions.

Switching to a smaller scope:

  • Gives you a chance to assimilate feedback.

  • Gives you a chance to make a more highly leveraged decision.

For example, in our “add 3 parameters” scenario, switching to the calling function (in this case a smaller scope because it is only one line):

  • Generates feedback. What if we don’t have access to the value we need to pass? We may need to abandon this refactoring.

  • Realizes the value. We decided to add a parameter, now we’re doing it.

Moving back to the called function:

  • Assimilate feedback. We can now decide with greater clarity whether to continue the refactoring.

  • Highly leveraged decision. Now without being in the weeds about how we’re going to pass a parameter we can decide whether & how to continue.

For a larger example, moving from task to coding:

  • Generates feedback about the true cost & value of the task.

  • Creates value in the form of either new behavior or better structure.

Moving from coding to task:

  • Gives us a chance to rethink our task decision based on our coding experience.

  • Gives us a chance to decide to modify, abandon, expand, or switch tasks.

Physical Expression

I realize the above may sound theoretical. I put it into practice with my chair-with-wheels. Literally. When I realize that I have been at too low a level for too long, I roll back so I can’t reach the keyboard. This cues my brain to start thinking at a larger scope. When pairing (I formed this habit pair with Ward Cunningham), rolling back is a cue that it’s time to start talking at a higher level. Conversely, rolling forward is a cue to move to a smaller scope, roll up your sleeves, & start making stuff.

Practice moving from scope to scope. Be explicit. Get in the habit of calling out scope switches verbally.

Goldilocks

Two ways you can destroy value when switching scope:

  • Too soon.

  • Too late.

Switching too soon is a problem for those of us who are Boredom Sensitive (aka ADHD). You’re not ready to switch to a larger scope until you have either realized some value (e.g. a new test is passing) and/or generated some feedback. You’re not ready to switch to a smaller scope until you either know what concrete thing you want to do next or know what question you want to answer.

Switching too late is the Sunk Cost Fallacy at work. “We just have to get this last bug fixed.” Yeah, maybe, but yeah, never. Roll back & think about this. Are we ever going to get all the bugs or is this code just too complicated? The phrase I use on myself is, “Hands off the keyboard. Nobody has to get hurt.”

Conclusion

Good luck becoming aware of scope switching. When it’s going well it’s just magic. Making just the right decision at just the right level at just the right time with just the right information. And now with the genie beside me that lever just got longer.
