Out walking this morning I came upon a little bird hopping along the sidewalk. It would try to fly, only making it a meter or two in the air before landing. Flying around it, chirping wildly, was a slightly larger version of the same bird. It was clear what had happened.

The little bird had left the nest prematurely. Thought it could fly but it couldn’t, not well enough to make it out in the world. Momma bird was trying to protect & encourage baby.

I captured it in my hands (move slow, grab fast). Handed it over to a neighbor who promised to care for it. But I didn’t feel good about its future.

Me

I’ve been feeling some kind of way about buying a house. The fluttering baby bird came uncomfortably close as a metaphor for that feeling. Am I really ready for this?

We’ll start, as usual when dealing with an engineer, with data. I don’t talk about the absolute numbers of my finances but relative measures will suffice. Each pair of stacks is a year, 2023-2025. The blues are revenue, the greens (? I’m red-green color blind) are expenses.

The highest level take away is that in 2023 I was a little below break even. I got ahead in 2024 (part of what let me pay the house down payment). In 2025, with the additional expenses of the house, I’m going to be a bit behind again. The challenge is that the down payment on the house absorbed most of my cushion, so I’m much more sensitive to any kind of downturn than I have been. That’s where the anxiety comes from.

Another takeaway is that sponsorships (the dark blue revenue) don’t have to grow much this year compared to last year to make up the shortfall. I was thinking I had to radically grow my business to get back above water but no, incremental growth will be fine.

Ergodicity

The strategic problem I have with my personal finances post-purchase is ergodicity—the ability to survive bad luck. I’ve talked about ergodicity (what a terrible name, right?) aka survivability several times, but it doesn’t hurt to repeat such a fundamental message. (Read ’s brilliant “Ergodicity” to dig deeper.)

The basics. We have a process that generates money:

But, unless you’re Bernie Madoff, no money making process is this smooth. There some variation around the growth.

Here’s the catch, the proviso risk-taking humans seem to miss—if we ever hit $0, the game stops. As Luca puts it, “Game over absorbs future profit.” It doesn’t matter how steep that average growth curve is if we can’t follow the curve.

When evaluating an investment we need to simultaneously consider:

  • The expected return, how much money we expect to earn over time.

  • Our bankroll, how much loss we can absorb & still be in the game.

  • The volatility of that return, which along with the bankroll tells us how likely we are to realize the return.

My House

When I had 2 years worth of income in the bank, if everything went well, then everything would go well. If my revenue dipped for some reason, though, I had plenty of time to shift to another strategy—get a job, sell paintings, whatever.

With most of my cash reserves transformed into the down payment on the house, I no longer have that time in reserve. If things go badly for my business, I have to move out of my house & sell it quickly (which may or may not be possible depending on the market).

Compounding my sensitivity to volatility is the increased expenses of a house. My monthly baseline spending just went up by about 30-40%. That drags down my “Kent’s profit” curve, making me more susceptible to variance longer.

Finally, in six months I face a big tax bill on the money I earned to make the down payment. So not only do I have to earn more just to stay even, I have to earn even more than that to accumulate towards taxes.

I’ve gamed it all out & if everything goes fine I’ll be fine. But if you’re wondering why you see more commercial activity here, that’s why.

Fallback

What if everything doesn’t go fine? What if revenue dries up & I don’t make my payments or can’t pay my taxes? First, I have a few other sources of cash so I can put off the reckoning at least a year. But then:

  • I lose much of my investment in the house to transaction fees.

  • That eliminates progress I made toward retirement savings.

  • However, that retirement savings is insignificant compared to my fixed expenses, so I haven’t really lost much. It’s not like I can ever retire anyway.

  • I end up living with one of my kids until my finances stabilize at a new, lower level.

Which is bad, worse than I’m living now, much worse than I’ll be living in the new house, but not tragic. It’s not the streets.

First Flight

So that’s where I am. Six years ago, when I was at $0, I would never have believed I would be in a position to own my own place again. Now here I am, standing on the edge of the nest, flapping my wings, feeling my new feathers, excited by the possibilities, scared of splatting on the ground on my first flight & having some bald-headed giant pick me up & carry me off, but still excited at the thought of flying.
