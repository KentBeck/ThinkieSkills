In the previous chapter we saw the remarkable transformation option-oriented thinking brings to development—the greater the uncertainty, the greater the value of preserving & creating options. How do we create options? One way is investing in the design of the software, increasing cohesion & decreasing coupling.

Another way to increase options value is by slicing, being able to do work, get feedback, & bank improvement a little at a time. I want you to develop an intuition that slicing problems finer creates value. When faced with "do it all at once" versus "do it in little steps", I want you to bias towards the little steps. (Note this is a bias, not an inviolable rule.)

Options can inform this intuition, based on a quote from Nobel Prize winning economist Robert Merton, "A bundle of options is worth more than options on a bundle." What does that mean?

Portfolio Value

Say we have an egg & eggs are selling for $5 each. What happens to the value of our holding when the price of an egg goes up a dollar? We gain a dollar. If the price of eggs drops a dollar? We lose a dollar.

Earning a dollar is great, losing not so much. Can we refine this picture so we win when we win & we don't lose when we lose? Sure, that's what we talked about in the previous chapter. We need a call option, a piece of paper that allows us to buy an egg at a fixed price (we'll ignore premium & duration for the moment).

Bundles

We're risk-averse. We don't want to be exposed to egg prices. Or maybe we are ambitious & we want to be exposed to other prices too. How do we add silver bars to the mix?

I'm going to ask you to work through the following exercises. I'll give you a template to fill out. I'm highly resistant to doing this kind of work as I'm reading but I promise that if you do you'll learn something valuable. Unless you know it already.

First exercise: what happens to the value of our portfolio in all 4 case of price movement—eggs up or down & silver up or down?

Think about it. Fill out the numbers. Like really write them down somewhere. To learn, thoughts have to go all the way through your mind.

Here's the answer:

If prices change in opposite directions they cancel each others' effects, so the value remains the same. If both prices change the same way, then the effects sum.

We'd still like to win when we win & not lose when we lose. How about if we apply the same option trick to our bundle of egg+silver?

If you want to learn, work through the four cases. I've been doing this a very long time & I could feel my understanding deepening as I worked through them.

Here's the answer:

Huzzah! We have avoided losing should prices go down. That should be worth money. However, we're not satisfied. If the price of my egg goes up by a dollar, shouldn't I be able to profit, regardless of what happens to the price of silver? I made a good choice, I should reap the rewards.

We'll tweak our option strategy. Rather than a single option on our bundle of an egg & silver, we'll separate the options. Work through the payoffs.

And the solution:

If both prices go down both options are worthless so our portfolio value doesn't change. If both prices go up we profit on both options. If one price goes up & one price goes down, the option that wins wins & the option that doesn't doesn't cost us.

Bundles of Options

Now we can revisit the opening quote—a bundle of options is worth more than an option on a bundle. Importantly for the software case (you knew I was getting back to software, right?) the bundle of options is more valuable in precisely the case where one feature turns out to be valuable & another feature turns out to be a dud.

That's, like, all the time. We can't predict how users will react to new features. Some features are surprisingly valuable. Some are surprisingly useless.

The "bundle of options" model predicts that we create value when we create independent options on features. "Here are four features & I have to have them all together," is a less valuable statement than, "Here are four features & we could do these 2 first or those 2 first." Simply recasting our project plan into slices creates value. It creates value before finger has ever touched coding keyboard.

Creating & maintaining bundles of options is hard work. Software design, increasing cohesion & reducing coupling, is critical to creating & maintaining bundles of options. So are:

  • Strong relationships with people making product decisions.

  • Testing that predicts whether a change is safe.

  • Tool for rapidly deploying those smaller slices.

I receive feedback, particularly from younger programmers, that thinking like the above is "too abstract". I agree that it's abstract but I see that as a strength. When you try to make large-scale decisions while keeping all the small-scale details in mind, you can only scale so far before the decisions suffer. An abstraction like, "I create value when increase optionality, & independent optionality," let's me make large-scale priority decisions which I then quickly check with detailed reality. That's the intuition I wish for you.
