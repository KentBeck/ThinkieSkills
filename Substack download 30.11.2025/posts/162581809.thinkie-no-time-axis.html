Pattern: You’re stuck trying to understand or explain the relationship between two factors over time

Transformation: Ignore time & graph the two factors as the two axes

This Thinkie is a big departure. I haven’t said a word about visualizations so far, even though they are a big part of my thinking & communication strategy. I suppose my graphical Thinkies are so baked in by now that I hardly notice myself using them.

I claim absolutely no originality points for Thinkie No Time Axis. I stole it from Edward Tufte in his remarkable book The Visual Display of Quantitative Information. This book rewired how I thought. Get the hardback if you can afford it. It’s a beautiful piece of printing & seeing the graphs on paper makes a huge difference (one of the lessons of the book).

Later I may revisit some of my lesser used Thinkies from the book, but this one is immediately useful.

If you’ve been reading along, you will have seen the result of Thinkie No Time Axis. I was trying to explain why today’s (April 30, 2025) coding genies aren’t suitable for unsupervised operation. tl;dr they are good at adding functionality but terrible at design, so they end up painting themselves into a corner.

I started by using Thinkie Search Space:

The genie is good at going from one local maximum, navigating a bunch of complexity, & arriving at another local maximum. However, & this is the point of my argument, it does this by burning optionality:

This illustrates my point, but it’s hard to simultaneously show the genie’s approach compared with what I call “augmented coding”. I walked & pondered & walked & pondered for several days. I had clunky solutions with various colors or time flowing both ways from the current state, but they hurt more than helped.

Thinkie No Time Axis to the rescue! Plot optionality on one axis with functionality on the other. Here’s the genie’s path, burning optionality for ever diminishing functionality gains until no options are left:

Now it’s easy to visually contrast this approach with the empirical approach we talk about so much here.

We add some complexity implementing a feature, then tidy to add options, then again, & on & on.

The shift of frame has helped my thinking in other ways. For example, it’s straightforward to visualize the difference between expert & novice implementation of the same feature:

Part of what makes the expert an expert is only partly that they are better designers, better able to climb out of deep holes. The expert also gives themself shallower holes to climb out of. Reminds me of the saying, “The expert pilot demonstrates superior judgement to avoid having to demonstrate superior flying skills.”
