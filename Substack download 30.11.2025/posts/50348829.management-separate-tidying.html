We'll assume for the moment that you use a pull request/code review model (I'll argue for an alternative later). Where do you put tidyings?

Here's an ugly piece of tail chasing:

  1. I put my tidyings in with my behavioral changes.

  2. Reviewers complain that my PRs are too long.

  3. I separate the tidyings into their own PR, either before (more likely) or after the behavior changes.

  4. Reviewers complain that the tidying PRs are pointless.

  5. Goto 1.

The tidyings have to go somewhere or you don't tidy. Where do they go? Summary: in their own PRs, as few tidyings per PR as possible.

Let's go through the tradeoffs in more depth. Folks learning to tidy seem to go through predictable phases. In the first phase we are just making changes.


We're in the middle of fixing an if statement, realize a name is wrong, fix that, go back to the if statement. Change is change.

After learning the tidyings it's as if our picture of changes under the microscope snaps into focus. Some of those changes were changing the behavior of the program, its attributes as observed from the running of the program. Some of those changes, though, were changing the structure of the program. Those changes can only be observed by looking at the code. B=behavior, S=structure.


At this point there is still no plan, no flow between changing behavior & structure, just an awareness that there are 2 different things playing together.

After a bit of this, we start noticing the common flows. Chunking statements leads to explaining helpers leads to an easier time making behavior changes. Now programming is more like chess, guessing how the game will play out several moves ahead.

Note that we still have one big PR at this point. Now we're at step 1 of the loop above. Every move we made was intentional, aimed either at making easy changes or making those changes easy. Put it all together & it's a bit of a mess. Reviewers balk.

And so we split our changes into separate PRs. Sequences of tidyings (or even just one tidying solo) goes in one PR. Behavior changes go in a separate PR. If we switch back & forth between tidying & changing behavior, we open a new PR.

How you lump or split your PRs is a tradeoff. Think of it in terms of incentives. A big, all-inclusive PR shows a whole picture but may be too much for a reviewer to provide useful feedback on. Teensy tiny PRs invite feedback in the small, but at the risk of going off into the weeds.

Review latency is also an incentive. If code gets reviewed rapidly, then you're encouraged to create more, smaller PRs. Those more-focused PRs encourage even more rapid reviews. This reinforcing loop can run backwards, with slow reviews encouraging larger PRs, further slowing future reviews.

Once you get comfortable with tidying, with working in small steps, with working with absolute safety, I encourage you to experiment with not requiring reviews for tidying PRs. This reduces latency further, incentivizing even smaller tidying PRs.
