I just had the pleasure of reading “A City Is Not A Tree: 50th Anniversary Edition”. Contrarian architect Christopher Alexander noted in 1965 that urban planners treated separate neighborhoods as entirely separate. Planned cities like Brasilia had a sector for hotels, a sector for government buildings, a sector for recreation. You could draw a tree structure to describe such a city.

Such a reductionist approach is both factually incorrect & ineffective as a planning tool. A building can be, should be, residential and commercial. A street can be, should be, a path and a destination. A neighborhood can be, should be, a place to live and a place to work.

In Alexander’s metaphor, a city should be seen, not as a tree, but as a lattice. Elements of the city should be members of multiple sets at once. Such an analysis is harder, messier, more complicated than analysis when you assume that each element is a member of exactly one set, but multi-layered living provides opportunities for serendipity.

OKRs

Re-reading A City Is Not A Tree got me thinking about the reductionist, tree-like styles of management I’ve bumped into during my career. There must be something seductive about seeing a company as a tree, because people keep trying to apply the metaphor even when it clearly (to me, anyway) doesn’t fit.

Take OKRs, for a recent example. There’s an org chart. The leaves on the tree have OKRs. Add up all the KRs for the individual contributors & you have the KRs for the team. Add all the team KRs up & you have the org KRs. And up & up until you have KRs for the whole company.

Maybe the process runs the other way. The company has KRs which are parceled out among the orgs, which parcel them out to teams & so down to individuals.
