This is the first in a series on systems thinking. There is so much more to systems thinking than this, but these are the bits I use daily. I recommend Diana Montalion’s Learning Systems Thinking or Donella Meadows Thinking in Systems as good starting places if you want to dig in.

Pattern: you want to improve but instead you’re going backwards.

Transformation: look for a loop of reasons, not just a list.

Sometimes the cause of slowness is obvious. If so, there are other Thinkies (I’m looking at you Fun Bit & Easy Bit) to help you get started.

Other times, though, you don’t remember things being this bad. And the further you go, the worse it gets. That’s when I pull out Gerald Weinberg’s influence diagrams. Here’s my quick introduction.

  1. Draw the effects you are seeing.

  2. Draw an arrow between effects that influence each other. More of this one leads to more of that one? Draw an arrow.

  3. Put a circle on the line if the influence is negative.

Take newsletter revenue (Thinkie Self-Description). Subscriber revenue has stalled.

The paid newsletter market is getting saturated. The more subscribers I have, the fewer potential subscribers are still out there.

I read this as “more revenue leads to more saturation”. I find it helpful to state the meaning of the elements in the picture aloud. Saying the sentence catches mistakes I make in the direction of influence.

The more saturated the market, the harder it is to grow revenue.

Read this as “more saturation leads to less revenue” (revenue naturally diminishes over time as people don’t renew, an effect we could add to the diagram but not today).

Now we have our loop. Loops are powerful, more powerful than lonely causes. The more times around this loop, the greater the friction.

Here’s a trick—count the dots. Loops with an odd number of dots (like this one) are inhibiting loops (aka negative feedback loops). We’ll talk about reinforcing loops in the next Thinkie.

Within the scope of this diagram it’s pretty clear what might help—reduce saturation by increasing the size of the market:

Bigger market leads to less saturation, less saturation leads to more revenue. But then we could add to the diagram the limits to increasing market size. Or how more revenue could lead to more ads leading to a larger market. And on and on…

I find this kind of thinking powerful & leveraged but I can also easily go too far. I end up with diagrams of 15 or 20 effects & 20 or 30 influences & nobody gets anything useful out of that. I’ll talk more later about how I address the Curse of Completeness.
