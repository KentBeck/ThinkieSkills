The next section of Thinkies relate to project management. Taylorism has gifted us a set of dysfunctional assumptions about projects. Since these assumptions are mostly unspoken, it takes relatively little effort to come up with better ideas. (See Mary Parker Follett Dynamic Administration for an antidote.)

Pattern: the team is doing things in parallel

Transformation: perform the same tasks serially

Example: the team is implementing 2 features at the same time. Have the whole team work on one feature, ship it, then work on the next feature.

Here is where the limitations of the Thinkie format emerge. I could easily go on for hours or days about why this Thinkie “works”, when it works, its limitations, examples, & counter-examples.

For Thinkie purposes I’m just going to ignore all that. I’m going to assume that you are an intelligent, responsible adult. When you note the pattern you’ll apply the transformation & then you’ll evaluate the result. I’ve said it before—Thinkies are good for generating lots of ideas, not lots of good ideas.

Whether serializing activities will result in improvement or a disaster is up to you to figure out. The Thinkie has done its job as soon as it has suggested an experiment.

Don’t serialize everything. Consider serializing something that is currently done in parallel where the parallel tasks have unexpected interference.
