Before you start, I’m not advocating for anything here. I spent a large part of my career giving people permission to work on one thing at a time (run/right/fast, interface/implementation, behavior/structure). As I integrate genies into my work more fully, though, I notice myself doing something that looks like multi-tasking more & more.

This is my attempt to figure out what’s going on, what really changed, and to ask, “Am I kidding myself? Should I go back to single threaded work?”

Before

I would work for a while, programming or writing, then I’d need time to reflect on what just happened before I was ready to work some more. (“Reflect” for me often means “nap”, if you’re keeping track.) Eventually insight would come & then I’d know what to do next. (Did you know that “waiting” is a critical thinking skill?)

In the before times, reflection time was relatively short compared to action time.

Multi-tasking Considered Harmful When…

If I start another task during the reflection time, I’m going to pay the cost of remembering the context for the other task. Then I’ll be ready to go back to the previous task while I’m still in the middle of the second task. Confusion ensues. Better to just stick to one thing.

Genie

Augmented coding (or writing, for that matter) compresses the time necessary to do a “brainful” of work. It’s still the same brain, though, so the reflection time isn’t any shorter. I, or rather we, the genie & me, implement a batch of features & then we, or rather I, need to think about what to do next. More features? More design? More feedback?

The new rhythm gives me relatively more reflection time in my day. What to do with that time? I can only nap so much (glad to experiment with this if I can find funding).

Hello Again, Multi-tasking

What I’ve found myself doing resembles the bad old multi-tasking. I finish one episode with task 1 (say writing a book chapter). I need to think about what to write next, but that’s going to take a while. So I prompt the genie writing a Smalltalk virtual machine a few times. Then I’m out of ideas for that for the moment so I come write this essay (you didn’t think I was going to pass a chance for self-reference, did you?) Then I know what chapter to work on next.

It’s not bad old multi-tasking though. I’m not interrupting one action to take another. I’m putting reflection in the background as I always do, putting on another burst of action, & reflecting on that. I can have 3-4 reflections going at the same time. I’m used to that.

There’s definitely a skill involved to do this kind of juggling without getting lost. The tradeoffs have changed, though, & we have the opportunity to explore our cognition in new ways.
