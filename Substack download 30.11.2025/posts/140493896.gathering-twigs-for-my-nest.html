I started crying yesterday, seemingly out of the blue, while working on my girlfriend’s yard. The longing for a home of my own kicked me in the gut.

I’ve talked several times about how my goal of sustainability for this newsletter includes buying a house. Yesterday was the most raw emotional manifestation of that plan I’ve had yet.

Progress

To recap, my financial goals are:

  1. Meet fixed expenses without needing other side gigs. (Keeping the Mechanical Orchard Chief Scientist gig because it’s steady money, has an excellent chance of a long-term payoff, & I enjoy it.)

  2. Meet fixed expenses without needing other income. (Staying at M.O. but not having to.)

  3. Accumulate towards retirement (house goes here).

  4. Free from needing further income.

Towards the middle of last year I slowly came to realize that direct subscription revenue, while growing, was not going to register on the scale of my above goals. I made enough to guilt myself into finishing Tidy First?. I made enough to make my life a little better. At this rate, though, I wasn’t going to make enough for me to subtract anything from my life.

My frustration with the lack of progress & the lack of alternatives & the amount of time & energy I was spending on the newsletter started affecting my health. How many times do I have to go through this to learn the lesson? My skin is a highly sensitive antenna. When eczema flares up, there’s something wrong with my life. It got bad.

I started making changes to optimize the newsletter:

  • Tuned the about page, the headers & footers, inserted a Subscribe button in (most) public posts. Each change added a few paying subscribers.

  • Published the “Developer Productivity” collab with . This resulted in a 20% increase in paying subscribers in month.

  • Adjusted the balance between public and paid-for content. This had the beneficial side-effect of opening up the topics I feel safe talking about. After all, you’ve already invested (literally) in my success. Didn’t seem to affect paying subscribers at all.

  • Announced a price increase to match other professionally-oriented Substacks (to take effect January 15, current subscribers grandfathered in to their current price). Boom. Chaka. Laka. 33% increase in paying subscribers. In a week! So far!

Finally, I can see revenue progress that starts sniffing at the first of my sustainability goals.

That vertical line is obviously not going to go on forever. However, it represents 0.4% of unpaid subscribers, so there is still room for growth. My ratio of paying to public subscribers is still low by Substack standards.

Energetic & Economic Sustainability

One lesson of this episode for me is that sustainability is broader than I was paying attention to. I was looking at economic sustainability—can I afford, in dollars, to keep putting this much effort into the newsletter or should I switch to something else?

Noticing that I wasn’t making significant progress economically was a step in the right direction. My health pointed to another dimension of sustainability—energy.

Doesn’t matter if I’m making gobs of money. If my health goes, then I don’t have money or energy. I need to take care of both, or to switch to another activity that has a better chance of meeting my needs.

Glad to report that my health is under better control. Turns out doctors actually know things. Go figure.

I’m going to go through with the price increase. I don’t see any downside. If it tanks revenue growth because no one signs up at the new price, well, revenue wasn’t growing fast enough anyway. If it turns out that conversion rates stay the same, then revenue will grow faster.

I’m also going to explore turning Tidy First? into an online workshop. It’s a little frustrating to go back to the beginning of a growth curve, but that’s the way this game is played. When you start the growth curve, you don’t know how high it’s going to go. You have to paddle out to catch the next wave.

I’ll keep you posted.
