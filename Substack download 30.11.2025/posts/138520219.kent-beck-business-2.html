This is going to be a rather long & dark intro, I’m afraid. It’s necessary to explain where I am right now.

“I can’t wait for the chili!” S called brightly from her car as she drove past. I participate in a close-knit San Francisco neighborhood. The Halloween chili feast has been a neighborhood fixture for decades. Everybody gathers in costume, eats chili, drinks too much, catches up on all the gossip, & stumbles home happy.

We knew that this year was going to have a sad undertone. D, a long-time neighbor, had just died of cancer. She was my age. Last year she was fine—costumed, bright, energetic. January—diagnosis. October—gone. Everyone was going to miss her.

D’s passing was a reminder of mortality, a reminder to squeeze the juice from each day. It could all be taken away by next year.

Once the party started, “Where’s S?” “Oh, you didn’t hear? She died of a heart attack.”

Boom. Two days ago she was ready to party. Today she’s gone.

Somehow S’s passing hit even harder than D’s. This wasn’t a months-long, painful decline, with sad goodbyes, with poignant last-times. This was boom boom out go the lights. No chance for reconciliation. No closure. Just gone. And it could be me. And I’d never see it coming.

So?

Now I’m crying. You might be crying. Take a minute to breathe. What does this mean for my business?

I optimized my newsletter further last week. I wrote some decent prose to be delivered to highly engaged readers talking about how much I appreciate the freedom afforded me by paying subscribers & how I express that appreciation. Lo & behold, the numbers went up a little.

I had the familiar emotional reactions—frustration that I’m not already secure, shame that I’m trying to get something from folks, fear of being my manipulative grandfather. (Just because I realized how messed up I was about being in business doesn’t mean my problems were all solved. You’ve got to outgrow issues like this, not solve them, & that takes time.)

What I noticed also, & this is the real point of this post, is that I was periodically worrying about business during the day. I’d be walking along or typing along or cooking along & out would pop the worry bug. “Yeah, but is the newsletter thing played out?” “Yeah, but what if none of the investments pays off?” “Yeah, but how significant is a book anyway given your timeline & fixed costs?”

That’s what the sadness double whammy of this week teaches me—the utter futility of those thoughts & feelings.

Insert Logic

When I habitually do or think or say something that I want to change, my process is:

  1. Stretch out the time involved in the incident to see how it progresses.

  2. Find the false belief in that sequence.

  3. Formulate a piece of logic I can repeat to interrupt the sequence.

I expect to live a reducing financial lifestyle in the future. That’s just the numbers. Expenses (spousal support) stays fixed. Income (without changes) declines. This is not an irrational fear. It’s my arithmetic.

What is wasteful is worrying about it. I need to do what I can do given my situation & capabilities & then let the chips fall. In the meantime, live my life.

Stretching out the timeline of a moment of worry it looks something like: remember that I don’t know where February’s money is coming from → feel frustrated that I have to think about it → feel envious of friends who have financial security. The false beliefs?

  • I deserve not to think about finances.

  • Everyone else is comfortable.

(Identifying false beliefs is always uncomfortable. Some of the things I apparently believe are absolutely absurd.)

Inserting logic:

  • I deserve not to think about finances → what can I do about this right now? If there’s something to do, do it. If not, keep walking.

  • Everyone else is comfortable → I can only influence my own situation.

We’ll see if that helps.

Lessons

I take 2 lessons from my friends who died (& the ones with MS & the ones with Parkinsons & …):

  • Worry about finances is a waste of precious seconds on this planet. Those seconds aren’t just lost, the opportunity to enjoy or appreciate one of my many blessings is also lost. I could have good seconds & instead I’m choosing to have bad seconds.

  • Depriving myself today against an uncertain future is a lost opportunity. I could scrimp & save & still have it all taken away from me. All that sacrifice & no payoff.

  • But also, the mortality table says I need to be prepared another 10 or 20 or 30 years & I’d like those years to be as comfortable as possible. I can’t just blow it all today.

I guess this is the human condition:

  • A finite, uncertain lifespan

  • Needs to be met throughout that lifespan

  • No payoff for sacrifices that don’t play out but also,

  • Discomfort for sacrifices not made

Back to work.
