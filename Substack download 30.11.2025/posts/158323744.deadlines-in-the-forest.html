“You’ll find few deadlines in The Forest, few estimates.” I made this statement to an organization obsessed with “dead” lines. They weren’t too happy. Okay, smart guy, but what about a real deadline?

First Principles

Why do we care about time in software development? Partly because time is money. Less time, less money spent. But that’s only part of the picture, because the exchange rate from time to money, the Cost of Delay, varies wildly.

One source of varying cost of delay is uncertainty. The longer we work in an uncertain environment without feedback, the great the value we put at risk. Delaying feedback on a teetering tower of assumptions puts our investment based on those assumptions at risk.

But that’s not the scary cost of delay.

Real Deadlines

My friend Gitte is working with a large project to support viewing next year’s Olympics. Now there’s a real deadline. Launch February 1, 2026—big win. Launch February 23—disastro. The cost of those 3 weeks of delay is potentially infinite while delaying from January 7 to February 1 makes absolutely no difference.

So if you don’t have deadlines in The Forest, how do you deal with fixed dates? Same way you deal with any wicked problem in The Forest—a little at a time & in diverse ways. When problems are complicated & can only be addressed, not solved, redundancy is your friend.

All-or-Nothing Bets

What a project’s outcome is all-or-nothing, then “nothing” is one of the outcomes.

In The Desert, specifying a big project & then insisting on delivery of all of it is a sensible strategy, consistent with the rest of the beliefs & principles in The Desert. It is certainly one way to get crumbs of value from software. There’s always a big reset toward the end of the project, but if everything goes poorly you’re well-placed to distribute blame. This is The Desert after all.

In The Forest you want to reduce that downside because you’re going to experience it yourself. The best way to reduce the potential downside is to release a little functionality now.

Here’s the thing—those little releases aren’t going to be perfect & polished. They might not scale. They might have UI imperfections. They might not be easy to release. All of these are problems you’ll work on along with getting out the next small release. And the next.

But Parkinson’s…

Parkinson’s Law—work expands to fill the available time

Deadlines, & more specifically deadline pressure, is often justified by citing Parkinson’s Law. In The Forest, purpose trumps Parkinson’s. If everyone is working toward a shared purpose, then nobody wants to put any extra time into any task. Let’s get this done & move on to the next!

It’s hard to find, communicate, & sustain purpose. It’s much easier to say, “Three weeks? Ha! You have one week!” But deadline pressure throttles creativity & creativity is the source of outsized value in software.
