Two folks. A car. A sparkplug. Are they going to go bipping or not?

(For those of you not familiar, bipping is breaking car windows to steal whatever valuables are inside. It’s frequent enough in San Francisco that we call the shattered aftermath “San Francisco Snow”.)

I got into a back-and-forth with SF Supervisor Dean Preston about government responses to car damage & theft. His contention is that the way to reduce vandalism & theft is to teach tourists not to leave valuables in their cars. His argument strikes me as absurd, nonsense, a non-sequitur. Plenty of break-ins involve locals’ cars in residential & commercial districts.

Rather than point fingers or shout “neener neener”, I wanted to take a step back & ask what an effective response to this particular crime would look like.

Careful…

Most citizens have given up reporting car break-ins. The police will not investigate (for various reasons). The courts will not convict (again, various reasons). No restitution will be paid. I don’t trust police report numbers as an accurate measure of the problem.

Some folks say, “Just enforce the laws.” That won’t work. Even if I wave a magic wand & every bipper is instantly arrested:

  • The courts can’t handle the load.

  • The prisons can’t handle the load.

  • Long-term, the offenders will commit more crime, not less.

None of the simple solutions is going to reduce this or any other crime (or homelessness or drug addiction). Crime results from individuals acting within a system. If the system doesn’t change, the behavior won’t change. But the behavior needs to change.

The Criming Moment

Bipping will diminish when potential criminals decide not to smash windows. Do something else instead. How might that happen?

  • Too costly. For example, if cars used while bipping were seized, that would increase the cost.

  • Too dangerous. I’ve heard plenty of stories of frustrated citizens taking punishment into their own hands. However, vigilantism escalates violence.

  • Not lucrative enough. What if items once stolen can’t be turned into cash because fences have been put out of business?

  • Socially ostracized. Currently, bipping gives offenders a status boost in some communities. If instead bipping lowered status for offenders, they would be less likely to offend.

  • Locked up. If all the potential criminals are in prison, they can’t commit crimes. (This is one of those “simple” solutions.) Mass incarceration perpetuates racism & won’t stop the next generation of offenders.

  • Better crimes available. I’m not advocating for this, just noticing that if mugging or kidnapping were more lucrative & just as safe, folks might stop bipping. No bueno.

Conclusion

We all occupy this magical spit of land together. Everyone wants more hope & less fear. More joy & less sorrow. Crime pushes us away from our shared goals.

Excusing offenders doesn’t help. Only when they choose to do something other than offend will offenses diminish.

Ignoring the system in which offending seems like the best option also doesn’t help. Influencing systems requires taking a look at various factors, going up-stream of the moment of offense for interventions, staying aware of unintended consequences, & ignoring simple fixes that won’t help.

Unfortunately, it seems like voters don’t consider these factors when electing officials. As electors we need to do a better job of selecting people who work thoughtful for the public good.

In the meantime, I’d like to see some skin in the game. Mr. Preston, park your car on the street. Send out an update every time it’s vandalized. I dare you.
