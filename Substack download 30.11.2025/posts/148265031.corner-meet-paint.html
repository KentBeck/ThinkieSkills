I’ve been stalling on writing Tidy Together?: Team Empirical Software Design, about which more later. In the meantime I’ve been thinking about the difference between empirical design & speculative design, leading me to post this:

As you can see, it stirred up a fair amount of reaction, both affirmations & contradictions. What more can a provocateur ask for?

A representative con comment:

In large systems—like an e-commerce platform with multiple teams—early decisions on things like data models can prevent costly integration issues later.

To which I say—early wrong decisions on things like data models necessitate costly migrations later.

This is the tension—we legitimately want to make decisions early and we don’t yet have the information to consistently make those decisions correctly.

The empirical answer is:

  1. Wait on the decision while making progress. (This is the first skill.)

  2. Learn from the progress. (This is the second skill.)

  3. Go from the current local maxima to a higher one. (Really 3 skills—knowing it’s time, seeing the higher maxima, & navigating from here to there.)

Which leads to my sound bite:

When painted into a corner, the solution is in the painter, not the paint.

You can do this! You can be empirical as a designer! If you can’t, there are skills you can learn that will help you.

The Book

I haven’t published another chapter for 4 weeks because I haven’t drafted one. It’s not for lack of trying. I’ve pushed myself to write & just haven’t been able to complete anything.

At dinner with data wrangler extraordinaire Josh Wills the other night, I may have uncovered part of the stuckness—I’m no longer scared.

With TF? I was good & scared. I hadn’t published a book in 15 years. Could I even do it again? Did I understand the topic well enough? Could I scrape together enough executive function? Add to that the obligation of publishing for my first few paying subscribers & I stayed on task.

Now I’m not scared. Yes I can write a book. Yes I have things to say on the topic that folks find novel & helpful. No books aren’t a financial proposition, but with the newsletter they aren’t bad (remind me to do a business update soon).

I’m not scared. If I don’t push through, nothing bad happens in the short term. Long-term this is a terrible strategy, but I’m as prone to immediacy bias as anyone.

I need to find a way to either get scared or write without it. I’d prefer the latter, but we’re talking about a lifetime (so far) habit. I’ll keep you posted.

The other factor in book writing is scope management. The outline (I’ll publish an updated outline soon) is too long. Time to trim to something that y’all will actually read & I’ll actually write.
