(I used to assume everyone knew the story of Goldilocks & the 3 Bears. Protip: everybody does not. Here’s a short version. Feel free to skip to “Tradeoffs” if you know it already.)

Once upon a time, in a tidy cabin in the woods, 3 bears were cooking their breakfast porridge (porridge is like stew but boring). When it was in their bowls, it was too hot to eat. They went for a walk to let it cool. Isn’t thermodynamics wonderful?

Along came Goldilocks (I’m anti-blond-shaming, but tradition). Seeing cute ursine architecture & displaying a complete lack of boundaries, in she went to see what was what.

First she tasted the porridge. The first bowl was too hot. The second bowl was too cold. The third bowl was just right, so she gobbled it all up.

Then she sat in the bears’ chairs. The first one was too big. The second one also. The third one was just right, but it crashed to the floor under her.

Finally she laid down in the beds. The first one was too hard. The second too soft. The third one was just right & she went fast asleep.

Turns out there is a wide variety of ways to close this story when the bears return, from sweet & cute all the way to horror/slasher. I leave the ending to you because we have enough for our purposes.

3 Tradeoffs

Let’s take a closer look at the decisions Goldilocks makes, not because we care, but because we make structurally similar decisions every day & we’re trying to get better at making them.

Porridge

We’re going to draw a diagram to help us think.

First we have a criteria for success. By convention we draw this vertically with better towards the bottom & worse towards the top (it’s a puzzle why this is my consistent bias, a puzzle I’m trying to work through by writing for you).

The next step is to decide on a minimum level of yum (or maximum yuck, whatever works).

The next step is to introduce the input parameter, in this case heat.

Now we add our mouth’s response to too much heat.

(We won’t go through all the steps for all the tradeoffs, just the interesting bits.) Now the countervailing effect—too cold is yuck.

Next we can sum the two curves.

Extending the “acceptable” line gives you an acceptable range for heat.

And there we have it, not too hot & not too cold.

Bed

The bed case is similar, so similar that I’ll only point out the challenge of interpreting the cost. The cost of the bed being too hard is that it hurts to sleep on. The cost of the bed being too soft is Goldilocks feels smothered.

This is a good example of how the curves aren’t fixed. Me with my bad back might have a lower curve on the right side—if I don’t have a hard bed it will be more uncomfortable for me. Someone else might need a software bed, so their curve will also be a different shape. Different shapes lead to different acceptable ranges.

Chairs

The chairs tradeoff is a little different. There isn’t an obvious single control dimension. Instead, chair design goes from something like “little & delicate” to “big & sturdy”. Another difference is that there isn’t a solution in the bears’ house to the chair tradeoff, unlike the porridge & the bed.

A solution here would be something like “de-couple size & sturdiness”.

Conclusion

Here we have the basics of the tradeoff structure we will use for the rest of this book:

  1. A payoff dimension, with better generally going down in the graph.

  2. A minimum acceptable payoff.

  3. A control dimension varying between 2 extremes.

  4. Two counteracting payoff curves that can be summed.

  5. Resulting in an acceptable range for the control dimension.

I’ll say this approximately a million times in this book: don’t be fooled by the precision implied by the curves. We are trying to inform our gut here, not make a mathematically precisely optimal decision. (Later we’ll get into exactly where we add value to a decision.)
