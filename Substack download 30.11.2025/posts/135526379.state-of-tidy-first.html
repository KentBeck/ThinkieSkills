Why is it good for you to pay for this newsletter?

My initial purpose in offering paid subscriptions was a trade—I would get an incentive to keep writing & subscribers would get early access to material.

✅ This worked for me. I got the draft done, wrote consistently, received valuable feedback that helped me refine both the outline & the chapters as they appeared.

What I didn’t expect was that I would kinda make money. So far I can buy a car, not a house, but I’ve talked with other Stackers who have bought houses from their writings. So now I’m at a decision point. Do I:

  • Ramp up & see if I can make this my sole income?

  • Let it bump along & enjoy the modest monthly bonus?

Empathy

I was describing my dilemma to my business coach & they asked a great question—why is it good for people to pay to subscribe? I had been looking at it from my perspective—incentive to write, income, feedback. But what about you all? Why is it worth not just your time, but your money, to read what I write here?

(Actually let’s just treat that as a real question. I’d love to see your answers in the comments. It’s safe. You’ll be among other paid subscribers.)

I’ll make up what I think might be the answer. (This is all preliminary thinking, if that didn’t come through yet.)

Stepping back, I assume we all believe:

  • Software design matters. That is, if folks were better at software design there would be more value & greater joy in the world.

  • Software design is under-understood. That is, folks could learn a lot quickly if the material was presented better.

  • As a geek, software design feels good. Our pattern matchers get tickled when we see which direction to take a system & break down how to get there into small, safe steps.

The most direct way to respond to this set of beliefs is to:

  • Practice software design

  • Improve your practice of software design

  • Pass on what you know

This sort of personal approach hits limits, though. You can only pair with so many people. If you’d like to see software design & the benefits of software design spread to our entire profession, supporting Tidy First? is one more thing you can do to make that happen.

An additional benefit I’ve seen start to emerge is a community in the comments. Other folks are increasingly responding to each other.

So, there’s my answer—you pay for Tidy First? because you care about software design & you’d like to see software design expertise spread further faster. But I’m still interested in your actual reasons.
