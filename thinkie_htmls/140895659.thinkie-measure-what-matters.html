Trigger: you are measuring the steps of a multi-stage process but you aren’t seeing the large-scale improvement you want.

Transformation: measure the value you actually want to change, even if that measure is vague or not actionable or complicated.

If you care about profit, measure profit. If you care about customer satisfaction, measure customer satisfaction. If you care about employee retention, measure employee retention.

Measurement appeals to our need for certainty & predictability. However, most measures the provide a sense of certainty & predictability are illusions. We all operate in complex contexts. Cause & effect have no obvious connection & are anyway subject to variance, reinforcing & inhibiting feedback loops, & measurement error.

When I’ve succumbed to the siren call of micro-optimization, when I’ve then become frustrated at the lack of overall progress, when I’ve started thrashing, then it helps me to take a step back & look at the bigger picture. What is it I actually want?

I used this Thinkie recently as I dealt with my frustration about this newsletter. I had been measuring total historical revenue—it’s great compared to just publishing a book but I was running out of energy & feeling financial pressure. Then I was measuring daily revenue, which had tons of noise & anyway didn’t relieve the financial pressure.

What matters? Am I reliably making enough so I don’t have rely on bursty (& recently diminished) consulting/speaking income? My current monthly run rate is $X. I need it to triple. MRR changes more slowly than daily revenue, it’s true. It doesn’t show the effects of today’s brilliant (or dud) post. But it’s what matters.
