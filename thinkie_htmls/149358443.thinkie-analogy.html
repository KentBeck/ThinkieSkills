Pattern: you’re struggling to explain a concept

Transformation: explain it in terms of another domain your listener already understands

Wow! This seems so simplistic when written down like this, but if it’s really so simple then why don’t more people do it?

Here’s the thing—analogizing gets easier with practice. And the Thinkie Motto applies—generate then evaluate. It’s like this, it’s like that, maybe it’s like this other. Get in the habit.

Analogies come easier to me at some times than others. My mood affects my ability to make analogies. Recently I was on a course of steroids & part of the general heightened energy was an easier flow of analogies. I talked with a writer who claimed that marijuana use encouraged analogies.

A bit of nerdy background, from “Metaphors We Live By” by Lakoff & Johnson—analogies (& similar figurative language) have these components:

  • Source domain—the (usually concrete) domain we are using for an explanation.

  • Destination domain—the domain we are explaining.

  • Mapping—the way the elements of the source domain map to elements of the destination domain.

  • Entailments—the way the mapping leads to understanding of the destination domain given understanding of the source domain.

Analogies are always a dance of is & is not. We don’t mean that a software schedule is actually a highway map. Some ways yes, some ways no. Part of the problem comes when we take an analogy so far that the mapping becomes absurd.

I had a conversation the other day about roadmaps (you know, the software project management kind). The roadmap analogy depends on the roads not changing much. In software we have collapses & washouts & new construction all the time. I said the plan should describe the destination, not the roads.
