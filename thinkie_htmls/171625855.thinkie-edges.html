Pattern: You aren’t getting enough resources coming in externally to be successful

Transformation: Increase the interaction of different kinds of resources

Okay, this Thinkie is pretty abstract as stated. Let’s start with an analogy. If you have a pond, would rather it be round or irregular?

The temptation is to make the pond round. After all, the round pond maximizes the efficiency of the excavator. (This is the mistake of valuing “simplicity” in XP, about which way more later.) Here’s the thing.

When you live at the edge between two biomes, you have access to two sets of resources. Riparian (“on the river bank”, but you probably already knew that) flora & fauna can draw on both water-based & land-based resources. Insects can eat on land but escape under-water to avoid predators.

As a designer, increase the irregularity of the length of the interface between elements. Have programmers frequently talk to real customers. Have back-end engineers collaborate intimately with front-end engineers. Pair between designers & programmers. Increase the frequency of whole-team planning.

Edges sacrifices micro-optimization for macro-optimization, one of the hardest tests of courage in software development. “If I just did this myself, I’d be done already.” Yeah, but it’s not how much you get done, it’s how much we get done.

Back to the above statement of the Thinkie—sometimes we are starved for resources. It happens. Someone expects us to make the proverbial silk purse out of the equally proverbial sow’s ear. Can’t be done. But we’re not stuck. Oh no.

As designers, we can encourage encourage more or fewer edges. More edges doesn’t magically create more resources, but it does conjure the magic of collaboration. We can have the effect of more resources if we discover harmony where it wasn’t expected. The programmer says, “That would be easy to implement if only we…” or the designer ships an experiment that users unexpectedly love. You have the effect of more resources without needing more resources. All because you had the guts to increase edges instead of decrease them.
