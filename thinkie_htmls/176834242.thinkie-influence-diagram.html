Pattern: you have a big, complicated system that’s acting up & you’d like to regain influence over it

Transformation: diagram the effects you observe in the system & their influence on each other

Thinkie: Influence Diagram follows from Thinkie: Separate Observation From Judgement. We aren’t rushing to take action. More probably we have stepped back from taking actions that weren’t working. Our actions may have even been counter-productive—we found ourselves getting less of what we wanted instead of more. What’s going on?

We need to know:

  • The parts (especially the parts we weren’t aware of)

  • How they relate (especially the connections we weren’t aware of)

Exercise

This newsletter has become the surprise foundation of my work. Its success, both financial & in terms of audience, lets me keep doing what I love doing helping geeks feel safe in the world. But where to go from here?

I want to get to sustainability. I don’t need numbers for it, although that can be good. Should I make more newsletter content publicly available? I’ve gotten contradictory advice on this question.

  • Less public material incentivizes paying subscriptions.

  • More public material encourages overall audience growth.

So what’s going on here? Before acting, let’s understand as much as we can. More public material makes sponsorships more valuable. I get roughly a quarter million views a month. Double that & I could raise my sponsorship rates. More revenue means more sustainability.

Of course that’s not the whole story. More private material makes it more attractive for folks to pay for a subscription. My free→paying conversion rate is low compared to similar newsletters. Maybe it’s because I give away too much stuff (wouldn’t be the first time).

Of course that’s not the whole story. Saying I want to help geeks feel safe in the world & then turning around & advertising products makes some (vocal) geeks feel unsafe, reducing my subscriber revenue.

And we could go on & on—adding new observations, adding new connections, starting over from different perspectives, telling different stories, comparing the effect & time lage of various connections, & finally coming to some conclusion about which experiment to try next.

Why This Works

One kind of stuck thinking grows from frustration—the situation is bad & everything I do makes it worse. I can’t understand. Ima just sit here.

Influence diagrams (which I learned from Weinberg) unstick me by forcing me to ask more precise questions about my situation, to choose new perspectives from which to view my situation. Once I come to a genuine, specific conundrum, then I have something to do. I am unstuck.

(In the example above I wonder if I can do sponsorships in a way that builds instead of erodes trust.)

We’ll get into the Thinkies for reinforcing & inhibiting loops next & how to intervene, but thinking in terms of effects affecting effects is the foundation.
